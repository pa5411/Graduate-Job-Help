Video Tests

This is an assessment where you are given several questions and are required to answer the questions by recording your responses on video. There is no human present whilst you record your responses and therefore they can be completed at any time before the given deadline. At a later date, a human and/or AI (Artificial Intelligence) algorithm will then grade your assessment.

Video assessments may be independent assessments or integrated as part of a wider set of assessments which may additionally include numerical and situational components. They may be carried out directly through the company website or through a third party solution such as HireVue. You can typically use a PC, Mac, laptop, tablet or phone to do the recording.

General structure of video assessments

Promotional content

Some companies include promotional videos both before and during the assessment. These videos may also describe what you can expect during your assessment as well as provide you with general tips and advice.

Before the assessment

You will often be provided with an opportunity to get used to the technology by answering a practice question before you begin the assessment. Answers to practice questions are usually not checked, but you should confirm this by reading through the instructions that you have been given. After you are provided with the practice question, you have a certain period of time to prepare a response. In this guide this will be referred to as the "preparation time". After the preparation time has ended, you are required to begin recording your response. In this guide this will be referred to as the "response time".

Usually the practice questions will be of a similar format to the assessed questions. Therefore if the preparation and response times for the practice questions are 3 minutes and 1 minute respectively, it is reasonable to assume that the assessed questions will also have the same format. However this is not a guarantee, so as stated before, read the given instructions carefully.

Note that a question may contain multiple parts to it. For example, you may be asked "Why have you applied for the company. In your response, think about the role, your interests and your career ambitions".

You may be offered the opportunity to answer several different practice questions multiple times. Alternatively, you may only be offered an opportunity to answer one practice question multiple times. Use the practice question(s) to adjust your webcam, audio and lighting settings accordingly.

During the assessment

There may be several assessed questions. The most restrictive preparation time you may face will be 30 seconds whilst the most restrictive response time you may face is 60 seconds. Many companies provide longer times, for example, 3 minutes to prepare and 5 minutes to respond. The majority of companies will only allow you ONE opportunity to record an answer. A small number of companies provide two opportunities or more for you to record an answer. You may be able to take a short break between answering each question. However, it may not be possible to close the assessment and restart it at the same point. It is therefore advised to complete the assessment in one sitting.

After the assessment

It is unlikely you will receive human feedback from your assessment although you may receive feedback generated by an AI system. You should receive an email informing you whether you have passed or failed the assessment. Unfortunately the emails may not always be clear. For example, if you receive a rejection email, it may mean that you failed the video assessment. Alternatively, it may also mean that you passed the video assessment but your overall application including all tests up until this point did not meet the required threshold. Similarly, an email which states that you have passed does not always guarantee that you will proceed to the next stage of the application process, again because the required threshold may not have been met.

How to prepare

Many video assessments ask competency based questions. For specific advice about stength, competency and technical questions, refer to the interview guide.

General recording tips

  • If there is both a video and situational assessment, use a device with a keyboard to complete written tasks more easily.
  • Always wear formal clothes unless explicitly told otherwise.
  • Make sure you are in a well-lit room so that the assessor can see your eyes clearly.
  • Avoid distracting objects in the background such as posters. Use a clear background such as an empty wall instead.
  • Ensure that your recording device is fixed on a flat surface so that it cannot move during the assessment.
  • Charge any devices before you begin the assessment.
  • Practice recording videos on the device that you will use before you begin the assessment.
  • Familiarise yourself with the recording software and its settings.
  • Look directly into the webcam when recording your answer.
  • If you wear glasses, limit glare from the device display by reducing its brightness setting. Ensure that your glasses are clean.
  • Avoid looking into the webcam from unusual angles. Make sure that your entire face is visible and fills a good portion of the frame.
  • Avoid talking too quickly or too slowly. Take pauses where necessary. If possible, get feedback from another individual.

Tips on the video structure

  • For questions with multiple parts, if possible, display the question as you are recording your answer.
  • Read any correspondance carefully. They may give guidance on the format and questions that you will face.
  • Similarly, read the company career website carefully, as it may also contain useful information.
  • Check websites or social media such as TheStudentRoom, Glassdoor or /r/UKJobs where other users may share their experiences.

Typical questions

  • Tell us about yourself and your experience at university.
  • What interests you or excites you about joining our company?
  • How has the company been transformed by technology [X]?
  • Why would you like to work for our company?
  • What three reasons attracted to you to the opportunity?
  • You have chosen to apply for role [X]. How will your skills, knowledge and previous experience make you successful in this role?
  • What roles have you applied for and what is your preference?
  • How will you work with new colleagues to ensure a successful outcome?
  • How would you introduce yourself to your senior colleagues?
  • Describe a situation where your decision or opinion was questioned by others.
  • Describe a time where you had to make an individual change their decision.
  • Describe a time where you had to give constructive feedback to another individual.
  • Describe a time you solved a problem with a colleague by showing compassion and understanding of their perspective.
  • Describe a time when you had to build a relationship with another person to deliver on a project.
  • Describe a time you had to build a positive relationship with another individual to solve a problem or improve a result.
  • Describe a time you were given a number of tasks to complete within a short period of time.
  • Describe a time when you had to use different sources of information before making a decision.
  • Describe a time when you had to learn a new skill.
  • How would you go about learning new information?
  • What would you do on a task if you had limited information or knowledge?
  • What would you do after a setback on a project to ensure a good outcome?
  • [Given a particular task] How would you approach this task?
  • [Given a particular task] How would you respond to individual [X]?
  • How can the team apply solution [X] to solve problem [Y]?
  • How is technology [X] shaping industry [Y]?
  • Describe any personal, work or university achievement and what they mean to you.
  • What is your biggest achievement to date and why?
  • What is the greatest challenge you have faced and how did you overcame it?

Tips to answer the questions

  • Avoid reading answers from a script. Use prompt cards or notes which are out of shot instead.
  • Be prepared to give short but detailed answers. Avoid sharing unecessary details.
  • If you cannot think of an answer, use a hypothetical example instead.
  • For multiple part questions, respond to each part of the question in the same order as it is given.
  • If you freeze, take a deep breath and either start the response again or continue with your original response.
  • For competency based questions, use the STAR approach. See here for guidance.
